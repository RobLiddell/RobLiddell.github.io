Nonlinear Analysis of Nano- and Micro- scale Topographies on Dental Implant Anchorage Tested in Shear and Tension

By: Robert Liddell

2017

Goal

Determine the effect implant topography has on the growth of bone anchorage.

Background

Dental implants are treated in many different ways in an attempt to improve bone growth around them and increase bone anchorage. The better the implant is anchored by bone the less likely the implant is to experience mobility and loosen with time which would require revision. A commonly employed method of implant treatment are additive or subtractive process which increase implant topographical complexity. This study looked at the effect of adding nanoscale features to a microtopographically complex surface. Furthermore, a majority of studies focus on a narrow time scale. This study looked at implant anchorage over a 6 month time frame.

Summary of Data

The study was designed to place 240 implants (120/implant group) into 120 rats. with each implant being tested twice due to the lateral and medial arches this would result in 480 different test files if all samples were tested. These 480 different tests would be split between the 2 testing methods (Shear/Tension), 2 implant types (BAE/BAE+DCD), 2 arches (Lateral/Medial), 2 animal sides (Left/Right) and 6 time points (5/9/14/28/84/168 days). To simplify analysis this study will use the mean of the two arches, dropping samples which do not have both arches.

Growth Curves

Plotting the mean of anchorage (Figure 2.) of each time point on a linear axis for time we can note that after an initial rapid increase in anchorage, growth rate slows and appears to reach a plateau. This is mirrored in Figure 3. which indicates the mean growth rates between each time point.

The growth rates show that there is an initial peak in the rate of growth in anchorage between 5 and 9 days, after which anchorage growth slows down significantly. While the trends between the two testing methods are similar the BAE+DCD implant has a higher peak growth in anchorage compared to the BAE implant.

Non-linear Modeling

This pattern of growth is characteristic of asymptotic growth. This includes a number of potential models but for simplicity I used the following function: \[Anchorage=C \times \left(1 - e^\dfrac{-Time}{\tau}\right)\] This function was fit to each experimental group individually in Figure 4. The horizontal lines indicate the value of C, which is the eventual plateau level of the curve. The dashed vertical lines are the value of \(\tau\) (tau), and indicate how quickly anchorage grows. Larger values of C indicate greater overall anchorage, and smaller values of \(\tau\) indicate shorter times required for this anchorage to be achieved. Analysis was one by taking 10000 bootstrap samples of the data and using the nonlinear least squares method to fit the data. C and \(\tau\) terms were extracted and used to calculate 95% confidence intervals for the curves of best fit (Figure 4.) as well as the individual parameters (Figure 5).

Conclusions

I found that the addition of nanotopography significantly decreases the time required for implants to become securly anchored through a combination of faster rate of healing and stronger overall anchorage.
